Here's something that blew my mind when I first learned it:

**Sleeping 6 hours can make you feel MORE rested than sleeping 8 hours.**

How is that possible?

It comes down to one number: **90**.

Every night, your brain cycles through 4 stages of sleep. Light sleep → deep sleep → deeper sleep → REM (dreaming). One complete cycle takes roughly 90 minutes.

Here's where it gets interesting:

If your alarm goes off in the MIDDLE of a deep sleep cycle — which is what happens when you sleep random amounts — you wake up feeling like you got hit by a bus. Groggy. Foggy. Reaching for caffeine.

But if you wake up at the END of a cycle — even after fewer total hours — you feel refreshed. Clear. Actually *awake*.

This is why:
- 7.5 hours (5 cycles) → wake up refreshed ✅
- 8 hours (5.3 cycles) → wake up groggy ❌
- 6 hours (4 cycles) → wake up decent ✅
- 7 hours (4.6 cycles) → wake up terrible ❌

Pop quiz: what's the #1 sleep disruptor in most bedrooms?

Not an old mattress.
Not a snoring partner.
Not street noise.

**It's temperature.**

Your body needs to drop 2-3°F in core temperature to initiate sleep. If your room is too warm, your brain literally can't start the process. You lie there, tossing and turning, wondering why you can't fall asleep — and it's because your body is stuck in "daytime mode."

The research is clear: **the ideal sleep temperature is 65-68°F (18-20°C).**

Most people keep their bedrooms at 72°F or higher. That's 5-7 degrees too warm for optimal sleep.

Here's what to do tonight:

**Drop your thermostat to 67°F before bed.** Yes, it'll feel cold when you first get in. That's the point. Your body will cool down faster, and you'll fall asleep faster.

Can't control the thermostat? Three alternatives:
1. **Stick one foot out from under the covers** — your feet act as radiators, releasing heat
2. **Take a warm shower 90 minutes before bed** — counterintuitive, but the rapid cooling afterward triggers sleepiness
3. **Use a lighter blanket** — swap the thick comforter for a breathable option

## EMAIL 4 (Day 4)

**Subject:** Why willpower won't fix your sleep
**Preview:** Systems beat motivation every time

---

{{first_name}},

You know what never works for sleep?

"I'm going to try really hard to go to bed earlier."

It's like saying "I'm going to try really hard to fall asleep." The harder you try, the worse it gets. Sleep doesn't respond to willpower. It responds to **systems**.

Here's what I mean:

**Willpower approach:** "I should stop looking at my phone in bed."
**Systems approach:** Phone charges in the kitchen. Not in the bedroom. Not on the nightstand. Not even in the room.

**Willpower approach:** "I'll try to go to bed by 10:30."
**Systems approach:** A "bedtime alarm" goes off at 10:00. When it rings, I start my 30-minute wind-down routine. Same routine. Every night.

**Willpower approach:** "I'll cut back on caffeine."
**Systems approach:** No coffee after 2 PM. The afternoon coffee is replaced with herbal tea. The tea is already in the cabinet. The decision is already made.

See the difference? Systems remove the decision. And decisions are where willpower fails.

**The 10-3-2-1-0 Rule is the best sleep system I've ever found:**

- **10 hours before bed:** No more caffeine
- **3 hours before bed:** No more food or alcohol
- **2 hours before bed:** No more work
- **1 hour before bed:** No more screens
- **0:** The number of times you hit snooze
