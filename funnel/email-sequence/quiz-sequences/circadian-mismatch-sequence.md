Let me explain exactly what's happening when you get your "second wind" right when the rest of the world is going to sleep.

Your circadian rhythm is governed by a master clock in the brain called the suprachiasmatic nucleus. It coordinates all your biological timing — cortisol release, melatonin production, body temperature, hunger. Under ideal conditions, cortisol peaks in the morning (energy, alertness), melatonin peaks in the evening (sleepiness), and everything aligns with the light-dark cycle.

For Circadian Mismatch Sleepers, this timing has drifted. Melatonin is being released 3-4 hours later than it should be. So while your body *should* be producing melatonin around 9-10pm and pushing you toward sleep, it's not producing it until midnight or 1am. The 10pm energy surge isn't a second wind — it's just your normal cortisol-melatonin baseline for that time of day. You're not "wired" — your clock is set wrong.

Why does this happen? Light exposure is the primary driver of circadian timing. Artificial light at night — especially blue-spectrum light from screens — tells your suprachiasmatic nucleus that it's still daytime and suppresses melatonin. Do this consistently and your clock drifts later and later.

The critical insight: circadian rhythms are highly responsive to cues. The drift that took months to develop can often be reversed in days.
